01 / Problem & Objectives

Why does single-phase PV destabilise the grid?

The rapid proliferation of residential rooftop solar PV systems — almost universally connected in a single-phase configuration — introduces uneven power injection across the three phases of low-voltage distribution feeders. This asymmetry leads to voltage and current unbalance, elevated neutral currents, increased line losses, and degraded power quality that threatens both network equipment and connected loads.

⚡

Voltage Unbalance

Unequal phase voltages arise when PV injects excess power into one phase, raising it relative to the others. Even a 2–3% VUF can significantly reduce induction motor lifespan.

🔄

Neutral Current Surge

Asymmetric phase currents driven by single-phase PV injection cause substantial neutral conductor currents — sometimes exceeding normal load currents — leading to thermal stress.

📉

Increased Line Losses

Unbalanced currents increase I²R losses in the distribution feeder and transformer windings, reducing the overall system efficiency and renewable energy hosting capacity.

🌐

Limited Hosting Capacity

Without monitoring and mitigation, distribution networks cannot safely accommodate high penetration of single-phase rooftop PV, restricting the energy transition.

Project Aim: To simulate, analyse, and quantify voltage unbalance on a 415 V LV feeder caused by single-phase rooftop PV injection using MATLAB/Simulink — across eight carefully designed operational scenarios — providing a validated foundation for future intelligent mitigation strategies.

02 / Methodology

A systematic six-stage approach

The project follows a structured simulation-based methodology, progressing from physical modelling through measurement, analysis, and validation.

STEP 01

System Modelling

Three-phase source (11 kV), Dyn11 transformer (200 kVA), RLC PI-section feeder (1.2 km), and unbalanced load groups modelled in MATLAB/Simulink Simscape Electrical.

STEP 02

PV Integration

Single-phase rooftop PV modelled as a 9.6 kW array (3P×6S, MPPT via P&O) with DC-DC boost converter and H-bridge inverter with LCL filter. A simplified controlled current source model is also used for rapid scenario analysis.

STEP 03

Phase Measurement

Three-phase voltage and current measurement blocks at the PCC (Point of Common Coupling) capture Vabc and Iabc instantaneously across all simulation scenarios.

STEP 04

RMS Calculation

Discrete RMS blocks compute stable per-phase voltage and current magnitudes (VRMS_a, VRMS_b, VRMS_c, IRMS_a, IRMS_b, IRMS_c) to characterise true operating conditions.

STEP 05

Unbalance Analysis

Voltage Unbalance Factor computed via IEEE approximation: VUF(%) = (V⁻ / V⁺) × 100, benchmarked against IEC 61000 (<2%) and NEMA limits. Symmetrical component decomposition applied.

STEP 06

Results & Validation

Eight simulation cases run with varying load and PV injection profiles. KCL verified at each phase node. Results compared against theoretical calculations and published literature benchmarks.

Key Finding Per Scenario

CASE 01 & 02 — BASELINE

Balanced & Unbalanced Load, No PV

VUF ≈ 0% VUF = 0.617% Ia = Ib = Ic

Establishes baseline. Even with asymmetric loads, VUF stays well within 2% IEC limit. Demonstrates that unbalanced loading alone — in this topology — is manageable.

CASE 03 — CRITICAL

Balanced Load + 20 kW PV on Phase A Only

VUF = 3.023% Va = 248.5 V ↑ Exceeds IEC Limit

Most critical single-phase finding. Concentrating 20 kW PV on one phase drives VUF 51% above the 2% limit. Phase A exports power back to the grid while B & C draw normally.

CASE 04 — IDEAL SCENARIO

Balanced Load + Balanced PV (13 kW × 3)

VUF ≈ 0% Va=Vb=Vc = 244.2 V

Near-perfect balance achieved. Uniform PV distribution raises all voltages identically; grid current drops to 27 A (from 41 A) as PV supplies most local load. Blueprint for ideal deployment.

CASE 05 — GRADUATED PV

Balanced Load + Graded PV (6/13/20 kW)

VUF = 1.624% Vc = 248.2 V

Stepped PV injection creates stepwise voltage profile. VUF stays below 2% limit but at 81% of the margin. Phase with maximum generation shows highest voltage rise — warrants monitoring.

CASE 06 — INTERESTING FINDING

Unbalanced Load + Unequal PV (6/13/20 kW)

VUF = 0.617% Partial cancellation

VUF identical to Case 2 (no PV). The unequal PV distribution partially compensates the load imbalance, suggesting that intelligently matching PV to load profile can neutralise unbalance.

CASE 08 — ADVERSE COMBINATION

Unbalanced Load + 20 kW PV on Lightest Phase

Phase A exports B & C draw heavily

Worst-case combination: PV injected on the lightest-loaded phase while heavy loads remain on other phases. Phase A becomes an exporter while B and C continue importing — maximising neutral current stress.

06 / Inverter Control Architecture

Closed-loop VSI control for phase balancing

The project also develops and simulates a full inverter-based phase balancing control strategy using a three-phase VSI (Voltage Source Inverter) with PI controllers — laying the groundwork for active mitigation.

🔒

Phase-Locked Loop (PLL)

Synchronises the inverter to the grid by extracting the angle (ω) and performing the abc→dq0 Park/Clarke transform. PLL parameters: Kp = 180, Ki = 3200. Lock time ≈ 44 ms (≈3 cycles).

📐

p-q Decomposition

MATLAB Function block applies instantaneous power theory (p-q) to decompose three-phase active power into balanced and unbalanced components, computing per-phase compensation targets (Pa_ref, Pb_ref, Pc_ref).

🎛

Triple PI Controllers

Three independent PI controllers — one per phase — process error signals (ΔP = P* − Pmeas, ΔQ = Q* − Qmeas) and output modulation indices (m) to drive the PWM stage for asymmetric compensation.

💡

PWM + IGBT Bridge

Sinusoidal PWM at 5–15 kHz drives the six-switch IGBT H-bridge (full-bridge topology). LCL output filter (2 mH, 47 μF, 1.5 mH) attenuates switching harmonics before grid injection.

07 / Industry Applications

Real-world deployment pathways

The simulation framework and monitoring architecture developed in this project directly support multiple commercial and infrastructure applications in the renewable energy and smart grid sectors.

Power Quality Monitoring Systems

Real-time VUF monitoring at distribution PCCs enables utilities to detect unbalance onset, log power quality events, and trigger alerts before equipment damage occurs. Directly applicable to SCADA and DMS platforms.

Rooftop PV Planning & Placement

The quantified relationship between PV placement (which phase, how much power) and VUF provides distribution planners with a data-driven tool to enforce phase-balanced PV installation approvals and net metering policies.

Phase Balancing Controller Design

The control architecture (PI + PWM + VSI) serves as a proven simulation baseline for hardware realisation of active phase balancers — commercially deployable devices that automatically redistribute load and PV injection.

Smart Grid & IoT Integration

The monitoring framework maps directly to IoT-based edge-intelligence platforms for autonomous VUF detection, logging, and compensation in smart LV distribution networks — aligned with KSEBL and similar utility SIH requirements.

Renewable Energy Hosting Capacity

By quantifying the VUF penalty per kW of single-phase PV, distribution operators can calculate the maximum additional PV capacity each feeder can host before requiring active balancing investment.

EV Charging & Demand Management

The same VUF analysis methodology applies to single-phase EV charging penetration — a growing contributor to distribution unbalance — enabling proactive infrastructure planning for charging networks.

08 / Key Findings & Conclusions

What the data tells us

Single-phase rooftop PV injection is the primary driver of voltage unbalance in LV distribution feeders. A 20 kW single-phase injection on an otherwise balanced 10 kW/phase system raises VUF from ~0% to 3.023% — 51% above the IEC 61000 / NEMA 2% limit. Balanced three-phase PV distribution achieves near-zero VUF (≈2×10⁻¹² %) — the definitive solution.

Finding 01

Single-phase PV injection is the dominant cause of VUF violations. Load imbalance alone (A=10, B=16, C=22 kW) produces only 0.617% VUF — well within limits — but adding 20 kW PV on Phase A alone pushes it to 3.023%.

Finding 02

Balanced PV distribution is the ideal solution. When 13 kW is injected equally on all three phases with balanced loads, VUF collapses to essentially zero and grid draw drops by 34%.

Finding 03

Intelligent PV matching can compensate load imbalance. Case 6 shows that matching unequal PV injection to an unbalanced load profile achieves the same VUF as the no-PV baseline (0.617%) — a key insight for smart controller design.

Finding 04

Voltage rise is phase-specific and directly proportional to PV injection magnitude. Phase A voltage rose 10.6 V (4.4%) when 20 kW of PV was injected — a measurable indicator for remote monitoring systems.

Finding 05

Neutral current is a critical secondary effect. Asymmetric phase currents from single-phase PV integration produce substantial neutral conductor currents, sometimes exceeding load currents, causing thermal stress on infrastructure.

Future Scope

The simulation model provides a foundation for inverter-based active phase balancing, edge-intelligence IoT monitoring, Volt-VAR optimisation, battery energy storage integration, and smart grid automation — all validated simulation pathways.
